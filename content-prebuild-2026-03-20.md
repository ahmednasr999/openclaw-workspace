## Post 2 - Tuesday March 24
**Topic:** Leadership/Mistakes
**Format:** Story with lessons
**Hook:** The 3 mistakes that almost killed my $50M transformation

I nearly blew a $50M digital transformation. Here's what went wrong:

**Mistake 1:** I hired consultants who knew the methodology but didn't know our people. 18 months of PowerPoint, zero progress.

**Mistake 2:** I chased executive buy-in instead of floor-level adoption. Every meeting was a victory lap while the team was drowning.

**Mistake 3:** I measured success by budget spent, not outcomes delivered. We finished on time and under budget. And completely missed the point.

The project survived. But I learned that transformation isn't about the technology. It's about who you become in the process.

What's the biggest transformation mistake you've learned from?

## Post 3 - Wednesday March 25
**Topic:** GCC Healthcare/Digital Transformation
**Format:** Experience-based insight
**Hook:** What 15 years in GCC healthcare taught me about digital transformation

After 15 years running PMOs across GCC healthcare, here's what actually moves the needle:

1. **Start with one process, not the whole system.** Nobody changes everything at once.

2. **Find the doctor who complains the most.** That person is your best ally. They've been living the pain.

3. **Accept that some executives will never get it.** Focus on the ones who do. Momentum beats consensus.

4. **Celebrate small wins publicly.** In transformation, invisible progress kills morale faster than failed projects.

The Gulf has something most regions don't: the ability to move fast without legacy bureaucracy. We're not using it.

What has your experience taught you about healthcare transformation?

## Post 5 - Friday March 27
**Topic:** Interview/Self-awareness
**Format:** Insight with question
**Hook:** The interview question that made me realize I was underqualified

"Is this your dream job?"

I used to answer that question instantly. Of course it was. I needed the job.

Then an interviewer asked me: "What would make you leave this role in 2 years?"

I froze.

Because the honest answer - "I'd leave if something better came along" - told me I wasn't passionate about the role. I was just passionate about not being unemployed.

Now I ask myself that question before any interview: What would make me leave?

If I can't answer it honestly, I shouldn't be applying.

What's your filter for deciding if a role is really right for you?
