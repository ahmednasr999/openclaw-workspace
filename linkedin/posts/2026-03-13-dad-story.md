My dad never understood what I do.
Last week he finally did.

He's 72. Retired engineer. Practical man.

For 20 years, every time someone asked him what his son does, he'd say: "Something with computers."

He wasn't wrong. He just couldn't picture it.

Last week, he came to visit.

I showed him one screen.
Not a presentation. Not a diagram.

A live dashboard showing patient flow across 15 hospitals in 3 countries.

Real-time bed occupancy. Clinical alerts. Pending discharges. Revenue per episode.

All feeding from systems that didn't talk to each other 18 months ago.

He stared at it for a full minute.

Then he said: "So you built the nervous system."

That's it.
That's the job.

I've spent 20 years building nervous systems for organizations.

At Intel, it was the network infrastructure connecting LTE towers.
At PaySky, it was Egypt's first SuperApp connecting payments, loans, and insurance in one platform.
At Talabat, it was the operations layer that scaled from 30,000 daily orders to 7 million.
At Network International, it was the PMO connecting 300+ banking projects across 8 countries.

And now: a $50M transformation connecting 15 hospitals across 3 countries.

Every one of those jobs had a different title.
None of them had a simple answer to "what do you do?"

But my dad got it in 30 seconds.

Because the job was always the same.

**Find the broken connections. Build the ones that matter.**

The title changes. The work doesn't.

What's the clearest someone has ever described complex work back to you?
