Why I hired PMs from banking, not from PMO backgrounds.
Most people thought I was wrong.

I wasn't.

**The Problem**

When I was building the PMO at Network International, I had 16 PM positions to fill.
300+ concurrent banking and payments projects across 8 countries.
High-stakes clients. Zero tolerance for delays.

The natural instinct: hire experienced PMOs.
People who knew PRINCE2, knew PMP frameworks, knew how to run a project register.

I didn't do that.

**Why PMO Backgrounds Weren't Enough**

Here's what I kept seeing from classically trained PMOs:

They were excellent at tracking.
Terrible at pressure.

Banking projects don't fail because nobody tracked the milestone.
They fail because someone didn't understand the client's risk tolerance.
They fail because a dependency in a payment integration wasn't flagged as the blocker it was.
They fail because the PM couldn't read the room in a tense client escalation.

Classic PMO training teaches you to manage a plan.
It doesn't teach you to manage a crisis.

**Why Banking Backgrounds Were Different**

Bankers understand urgency differently.
They've worked in environments where a missed deadline means a regulatory fine.
They understand that a client's silence isn't approval, it's a warning sign.
They communicate risk in financial terms, not just project terms.

And they ask uncomfortable questions earlier.

**What I Actually Looked For**

1. Evidence of managing ambiguity, not just complexity
2. Client-facing experience where stakes were real
3. Ability to escalate without drama and resolve without escalation becoming the story
4. Cross-functional coordination without formal authority

Banking PMs had all four.
And I trained them on the tools.

You can teach someone JIRA in two weeks.
You can't teach someone to stay calm when three clients are escalating simultaneously.
