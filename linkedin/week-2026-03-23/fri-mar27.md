A recruiter told me I was overqualified.
Here's what I said.

She was polite about it.
"Ahmed, your profile is impressive but honestly, this role might feel small for you."

I asked her one question.

"What problem is this role trying to solve?"

She told me.

I said: "That's not a small problem. And most people applying for this role haven't solved it before."

She went quiet for a second. Then she said, "Fair point."

Here's what "overqualified" actually means in most cases:

It means the hiring manager is afraid you'll leave when something better comes along.
It means they're worried about budget alignment.
It means they're not sure how to manage someone who knows more than they do.

None of those are your problem to apologize for.

**What I've learned after 20 years and multiple career pivots:**

Overqualified is a signal, not a verdict.

It tells you the person interviewing you hasn't figured out how to use your experience yet.

Your job in that conversation is to reframe it.

From "I'm too senior for this" to "I've seen this problem in a harder context and I know what breaks at scale."

I scaled Talabat from 30,000 to 7 million daily orders.
I led 300+ concurrent projects across 8 countries at Network International.
I'm managing a $50M transformation across 15 hospitals right now.

Was I overqualified for some roles along the way? Probably.

Did I take them anyway when the problem was interesting? Yes.

Because a title doesn't define the scope of what you're actually building.

The work defines it.
